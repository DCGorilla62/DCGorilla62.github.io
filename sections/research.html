Research

My research centers on the measurement of high-energy cosmic-ray nuclei using advanced detector technologies. I work on HELIX (High-Energy Light Isotope eXperiment) , a long-duration balloon mission designed to make high-precision measurements of rare cosmic-ray isotopes. These measurements provide insight into Galactic propagation, the history of stellar processes, and the structure of our local cosmic environment.

My contributions focus on detector calibration, reconstruction algorithms, and data analysis for the HELIX Drift Chamber Tracker. I develop software to model charge-division signals, perform track reconstruction, analyze isochrone timing, and evaluate systematic effects across the detector. I also work extensively with simulation tools and event-level data processing to prepare for upcoming Antarctic campaigns.

More broadly, I am interested in the intersection of particle astrophysics, precision measurement, and instrumentation. I enjoy building tools that make complex detectors function reliably in extreme environments — and translating those measurements into physical insight about our Galaxy.
